Self-Reference Criterion & Cultural Bias
Concepts related to cultural blind spots in international marketing decisions
Self-Reference Criterion (SRC)
▾
The unconscious tendency to interpret situations, behaviors, and business practices in other cultures using the values, experiences, and knowledge of one's own culture as the reference point. In international marketing, SRC leads managers to design campaigns and strategies that reflect their home market rather than the target market.
SRC & Cultural Bias
Cultural Bias
▾
The tendency to judge another culture using the standards and assumptions of one's own culture, often leading to misunderstandings or incorrect conclusions. Cultural bias differs from SRC in that it can be conscious or unconscious — it is a broader pattern of evaluating foreign markets through a distorted lens.
SRC & Cultural Bias
Ethnocentrism
▾
The belief that one's own culture, company, or way of doing business is superior to others, often resulting in resistance to adapting strategies for foreign markets. An ethnocentric company assumes that what works at home will work everywhere — and resists evidence to the contrary.
SRC & Cultural Bias
Cultural Projection
▾
The assumption that people in another culture will respond to products, marketing messages, or business practices in the same way people do in one's own culture. Cultural projection is SRC in action — it produces marketing that speaks to the sender rather than the audience.
SRC & Cultural Bias
Institutional Ethnocentrism
▾
A form of ethnocentrism embedded within organizations, where corporate policies, structures, and decision-making processes systematically favor the home-country perspective rather than local market realities. It is especially visible when headquarters overrules local managers who possess direct cultural knowledge.
SRC & Cultural Bias
Country Manager / Local Market Expertise
▾
Managers based in a specific country who possess deep knowledge of the local culture, consumers, regulatory environment, and business practices, enabling them to adapt global strategies effectively to the local market. When headquarters overrules local expertise, it often signals institutional ethnocentrism at work.

Hall's Cultural Framework
Developed by anthropologist Edward T. Hall — widely applied in international marketing communication and negotiation strategy
Communication Context
High-Context Culture
▾
A culture in which communication relies heavily on implicit messages, shared experiences, nonverbal cues, and the surrounding context rather than explicit verbal statements. In high-context cultures — such as South Korea, Japan, and China — meaning is embedded in relationships and situations. Direct statements can feel blunt or even disrespectful.
Hall's Framework
Low-Context Culture
▾
A culture in which communication is direct, explicit, and relies primarily on clearly stated verbal or written information rather than contextual cues. Low-context cultures — such as the United States, Germany, and Scandinavia — value clarity, brevity, and information delivered straight. "The product speaks for itself" is a distinctly low-context idea.
Hall's Framework
Explicit Communication
▾
Communication in which the meaning is clearly expressed through direct language, detailed explanations, and precise wording. Explicit communication leaves little room for interpretation — it is the dominant mode in low-context cultures and underpins advertising that leads with product features, pricing, and clear calls to action.
Hall's Framework
Implicit Communication
▾
Communication in which much of the meaning is conveyed indirectly through tone, body language, relationships, or situational context rather than direct statements. In high-context cultures, what is not said — the pause, the omission, the gesture — often carries as much meaning as the words themselves.
Hall's Framework
Relational Trust
▾
Trust that develops through long-term relationships, personal interactions, and mutual understanding rather than through formal contracts or institutional guarantees. In high-context cultures, relational trust is a prerequisite for doing business — brands that skip the relationship-building phase often find that technically superior products fail to gain adoption.
Hall's Framework
Time Orientation
Monochronic Time
▾
A cultural orientation toward time in which activities are scheduled sequentially, punctuality is emphasized, and tasks are handled one at a time. Monochronic cultures treat time as a finite resource — deadlines are commitments, meetings start on schedule, and efficiency is a virtue. The United States and most of Northern Europe are monochronic.
Hall's Framework
Polychronic Time
▾
A cultural orientation toward time in which multiple activities may occur simultaneously, schedules are flexible, and relationships often take priority over strict adherence to time. In polychronic cultures, being interrupted for a conversation is natural — it reflects the prioritization of people over schedules.
Hall's Framework
Context-Sensitive Communication
Context-Sensitive Communication
▾
A style of communication in which understanding depends heavily on situational factors such as relationships, status, environment, and nonverbal cues, rather than solely on the words spoken. Effective international marketers develop the ability to read and adapt to contextual signals — recognizing that the same message can land very differently across cultures depending on who delivers it, how, and in what setting.
