Chapter 5 · Case Study

YETI Enters South Korea

A premium American brand. A high-context market. A headquarters team that thinks it knows best.

SRC High / Low Context Hofstede Market Entry
The Brand
YETI is a premium American outdoor lifestyle brand known for its high-end coolers, drinkware, and rugged "built for the wild" identity. Founded in Austin, Texas, YETI built its reputation on extreme durability, aspirational outdoor culture, and a loyal domestic following willing to pay a significant premium for product quality. The brand's identity centers on individual toughness, self-reliance, and conquering nature — values deeply rooted in American cultural mythology.

YETI is now planning its first direct market entry into South Korea — a high-context, collectivist society where consumer trust is built through relationships, group identity, and social proof rather than through product specs alone.
The Austin Team's Launch Plan
01
Campaign Tagline
Emphasizes individual achievement, solitude in nature, and personal toughness.
"Be the one who goes further."
02
Communication Style
Minimalist visuals, no celebrity endorsement, no explanatory copy.
"The product speaks for itself."
03
Sales Channel
Direct-to-consumer via a sleek e-commerce site. No retail partnerships, no physical presence.
04
Launch Timing
Major campaign drop tied to a Friday release with a 48-hour flash sale window.
"Create urgency."
05
Customer Service
Fully automated chatbot. No human contact unless the customer escalates after three failed attempts.
06
Price Positioning
Premium pricing with no justification of price in any marketing material.
⚠ The Conflict
The South Korea country manager — a Korean national with 10 years of in-market experience — reviewed the plan and flagged it as "likely to underperform." The Austin team overruled the recommendation, citing the belief that YETI's global brand identity should remain untouched across all markets.
Think About This Before the Assessment
→Which decisions in this plan reflect American cultural assumptions being applied uncritically to a different market?
→South Korea is a high-context culture. Where does YETI's communication strategy assume a low-context audience?
→The country manager was overruled. What does that decision itself reveal about YETI's organizational culture?
→Using Hofstede's dimensions, which cultural mismatches carry the highest risk — and which single one would you fix first?
